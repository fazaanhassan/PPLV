UCL PPLV Research Area


The UCL Research Team is leading new developments into this field.
Particularly of note, Peter O'Hearn of the UCL PPLV research team developed Separation Logic which as has facilitated the development of programs to verify programs written in imperative programming languages. Members of the UCL PPLV research team were also directly involved in developing Facebook's INFER program, implementing separation logic which is used to ensure that phone applications can be developed quickly.

Key Researchers

For the sake of brevity, we've only been able to include a few of the main researchers in the UCL department. With a subject like PPLV, all research done in the area has significant impacts on the field of computer science, so we've selected a few of the researchers whose research we found particularly interesting.

Professor David Pym

The current head of PPLV and one of the foremost Researchers in this field, David Pym has played crucial roles through his proposition of Bunched Logic[1], a variant of Logic which has aided the development of verification tools. David is currently looking into Economic implementations of Computational Logic, as well as further researching into his areas of interest including Security Management, Logic, and Systems Modelling.

Impacts

As PPLV is centered around improving the logical concepts behind Computer Science, research done by PPLV has impacts over a huge breadth of fields. We've chosen to include a couple impacts that we found the most interesting below. Hopefully this should help you to get an idea of what the PPLV department does.

Gaming Market ( Boosted automatic verification Research Project )[3]


The core code (known as kernel code) for the Graphical Processing Unit (GPU)[4], is responsible for ensuring a high frame rate, smooth gameplay and a good visual appearance. This code is now being developed by automatic verification algorithms developed by researchers in PPLV.

The kernel code is the low level code required for the GPU to function - previously it was developed and tested by humans, however, thanks to PPLV, it is now being developed using automatic verification algorithms. These algorithms find a way to maximise memory usage and identify bugs faster than any human could.

These increases in efficiency may be one of the main factors in the UK gaming industry's recent economic growth - there is currently a high demand for GPUs.

Phone industry ( Program Verification Research Project ) [5]


Researchers at UCL also helped to develop Separation Logic and Bunched Logic, which are revolutionary new Logical models which allow imperative programs (written in languages such as C, Python, etc.) to be automatically verified.

The results of these discoveries are used by FaceBook in their INFER project, which is used to check phone applications for memory leaks and other problems. This facilitates the quick development of code for mobile devices. Currently there are over 250,000,000 android phones alone in use, all running similarly checked software, so it's easy to see how this development has actually shaped the modern world as we know it.

Business Sector ( Algebra and Logic for Policy and Utility in Information Security Project ) [6]

By producing a universal way of representing security issues within a system, managers within the industry can more easily model their policies to be sustainable, effective and affordable.

For example environmental policies such as recycling machines and paper and conforming to specific laws within a country. By using algebra and logic as a foundation to create models this addresses security flaws within organisations in relation to national laws, regulations and agreements.

Research Projects

As stated before, research done by PPLV can cover a wide variety of fields. We've included some explanatory videos on the projects we found the most interesting.

Resource Management

Developing automated methods of replicating the process done by programmers as they analyse the way their program handles resources or extending current computational logical models (separation logic, bunched logic) to other fields - such as using it to manage food or financial resources.

Productive Security

Developing systems to determine the actual effectiveness of security measures based off previous data - which unlike current frameworks in place accounts for the "missing link" variables such as employee workload and risk perception to more accurately predict employee behaviour.

Concurrent Program verification

Developing new automated methods of checking whether code is semantically correct (does what it's meant to) using Separation Logic. Current methods rely on hard coded identifiers for data structures which means that the verification will fail if an unknown data structure is found.

Algebra and Logic for Info-Security

Exploring the idea of how Algebra and Logic is observed within Information Security, in this case combinatorics of passwords, and how Policies may arise to essentially make unauthorized access to an account more difficult and hence secure.

What Next?

As you can see, PPLV is a research area under constant development by some of the best researchers in the world. It's research has huge impacts on how computers programs are developed and tested.
UCL's PPLV research department plays a key role in this field, with many of it's researchers having worked on projects that have literally changed the world. To us, as undergrad Computer Science students, it's humbling to see how approachable these foremost researchers are, and how willing they were to offer us advice on building this website.
Looking toward the future, possible new projects in the field of PPLV may be with relation to self-driving cars. With recent improvements in self-driving car technologies and companies such as Google beginning to create the first self-driving cars, self-driving cars seem to be just around the corner - however before we adopt this technology, we have to devise methods of ensuring that these algorithms will not lead to a crash.
