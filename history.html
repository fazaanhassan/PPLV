History of the Area[1]

To provide some context to the importance of PPLV, we've included a very brief overview of the history of PPLV, from the Birth of Computational Analysis, to the creation of Separation Logic.

Note: As PPLV is heavily focused on Logic in Computer Science - a history of PPLV would be incomplete without any mention of the history of Logic - thus, for the sake of completeness, we've also included important events in the history of Logic.

Let's get started...

The Birth of Logic to the introduction of Modern Logic

500 B.C - 19th Century

After initially being devised by Ancient Greeks as a tool for systematically determining the victor in debates, and then developed upon by later civilisations throughout the middle ages, the concept of Traditional Logic was established as a fundamental field of study. However, following a decrease in popularity over the 18th Century, Scholars studying recent innovations in the field of Mathematics, began developing the foundations of Modern Logic, which rather than focusing on producing abstract logical arguments, applied rigorous mathematical constraints to logic.

Advancements in Mathematical Logic [2]

19th Century to 20th Century

Following the development of the foundations of Modern Logic, further advances were made by interweaving mathematical and logical concepts (such as set theory). This also prompted an increase in the standard to which mathematical proofs were judged against - culminating in Godel's incompleteness theorem - which proved that certain (axiomatic) systems of logic would not be able to prove their own axioms, in retaliation to mathematicians of the time attempting to prove that every true theorem was provable.

Introduction of Boolean Algebra through to the development of Computers[3]

1854 -1937

Following the increasing levels of scrutiny to which current mathematical concepts were being considered, George Boole produced a new logic field, which in some ways attempted to formalize older logical concepts, such as Aristotle's system of logic, where the outcomes of expressions were only either true or false. During this period, Charles Babbage was investigating how mechanical systems could be set up to compute the results of theoretical concepts, the results of which culminated in his blueprints for a difference machine. Charles Babbage also worked with Ada Lovelace, who, based off her experience working on the difference machine, went on to construct the first ever algorithm designed for a computer - one to calculate Bernoulli numbers.
Throughout the 19th Century, research into electrical circuits increased, and scholars began to see how certain logical structures could recreate logical expressions - this culminated in Claude.E Shannon's Invention of Signal Flow Graphs (in his paper titled "The Theory and Design of Linear Differential Equation Mechanics"), which allowed boolean logic to be used to describe circuits.

Birth of Computational Logic [4]

1920 - 1944
Following advances in the complexity of such number-crunching machines, Alan Turing and Alonzo Church independently began formalizing the concept of an algorithm and were able to prove that provided it were capable of a certain set of actions, a computer could compute any algorithm given enough time and resources. Based off the findings of this research, Alan Turing went on to produce an abstract description of a computational machine, a model which determined which problems could be solved algorithmically.

NP Completeness and Formal Semantics

1960s - 1970s

Building on the analysis of what is and isn't possible for a computer to calculate, the concept of NP-completeness was developed by researchers in the US and USSR[5]. During this period, the field of formal semantics was established as a more rigorous method of describing the structure of human languages - the techniques developed in this field were then adapted to describe computational languages - this produced axiomatic semantics (describing programs by specifying properties which hold before and after a program has been executed) amongst other new techniques.

Logic of Bunched Implications and Separation Logic[6]

1999 to Present
In order to more accurately describe abstract computational systems, Peter O'Hearn and David Pym at UCL developed Bunched Logic (introduced in their paper "The Logic of Bunched Implications"). Bunched logic allows for the in-depth analysis of resource composition, which facilitates more accurate analysis of computer systems. This Logic model then allowed another group of researchers (including Peter O'Hearn) to develop Separation Logic - a system that is now frequently used in industry to verify programs which use mutable memory structures. This development has had a huge impact on the accuracy of automated verification techniques, increasing the overall reliability of modern programs.

Closing words

The concept of Logic is inherently present in all technological advancements.

Since the foundation of traditional logic by Aristotle, where every idea had to be either True or False, Logic has been increasingly embedded in the realm of mathematics and ultimately has become integral to the foundations of advanced technology.

Hence, a field such as Principles of Programming, Logic and Verification which is focused on the development of Logic is going to play a role of increasing importance in the modern world, as our technologies become more and more complex.

For example, considering the ever-increasing amount of information available, scientists and engineers are constantly seeking to improve the methods of processing data to keep up with the increased demand. Consequently, developments in PPLV regarding resource management have gained huge importance in the modern world.
If we want to sustain technological innovation, and develop faster and smarter digital systems, research into the field of PPLV is a must.
